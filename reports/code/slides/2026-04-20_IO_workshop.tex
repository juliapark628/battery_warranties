\begin{frame}{Motivation}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Grid-scale batteries have expanded rapidly in U.S. electricity markets
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Over 26 GW of installed capacity by early 2025
        \item ERCOT alone hosts approximately 3.2 GW (Reynolds, 2025)
    \end{itemize}
    \pause
    \item The primary economic rationale for this investment is \textbf{flexibility}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Unlike intermittent renewables or fossil-fuel generators with startup costs, batteries can respond within seconds
        \item This makes them uniquely valuable for arbitrage and ancillary services
    \end{itemize}
    \pause
    \item But in practice, batteries do not operate unconstrained
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Manufacturer performance warranties impose binding operational restrictions
        \item These contractual frictions may distort dispatch away from profit-maximizing and socially optimal behavior
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Research Question}
\textbf{Central question:} \\
\vspace{0.2cm}
\textit{How do cycling restrictions in grid-scale battery performance warranties distort dispatch away from profit-maximizing and socially optimal behavior?}

\vspace{0.5cm}
\pause
\textbf{Why this matters:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Batteries earn a disproportionate share of revenue on a small number of high-price days
    \item Rigid warranty cycle restrictions may prevent operators from cycling intensively on those high-value days
    \item If operators face forward-looking incentives to conserve cycles, distortions may be significant even on days where the cycle budget isn't fully binding
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\section{Institutional Background}

\begin{frame}{Context: Grid-scale battery warranties}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Commercial Warranties}: a short-term guarantee that covers defects, part replacements, and component failures at the beginning of life
    \item \textbf{Performance Warranties}: guarantee that the storage system will maintain specific performance standards over a longer period---often between 15-20 years
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Context: Grid-scale battery warranties}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textcolor{gray}{Commercial Warranties: a short-term guarantee that covers defects, part replacements, and component failures at the beginning of life}
    \item \textbf{Performance Warranties}: guarantee that the storage system will maintain specific performance standards over a longer period---often between 15-20 years
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Context: Types of Performance Warranties}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{End-of-Life State of Health (SoH) warranty}: Promises a specific SoH (typically 70--80\%) by the end of the warranty period if the battery is used within certain operating conditions.
    \item \textbf{Annual SoH warranty}: Promises a maximum yearly degradation rate if the battery is used within certain operating conditions.
    \item \textbf{Penalty-based warranty}: Allows for adjustments in the yearly SoH guarantee depending on how the battery is operated (e.g. if the system operates under sub-optimal conditions, the guaranteed SoH is reduced by a predefined formula).
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Context: Types of operating restrictions}
Restrictions imposed on battery operation to prevent degradation:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Cycle frequency limits}: cap on full charge-discharge cycles per day or per year (typically clustered around 1--1.5 cycles/day)
    \item \textbf{State-of-charge requirements}: constrain annual average SOC, commonly below 50\%
    \item \textbf{Depth-of-discharge (DoD) limitations}: prevent full cycling of the nominal capacity on each dispatch event
    \item \textbf{C-rate caps}: limit maximum power-to-capacity ratio (e.g., 0.25C on a 100 MWh battery caps power at 25 MW)
    \item \textbf{Resting period requirements}: enforce battery inactivity after a cycle to allow temperature regulation
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Context: Trends in battery warranties}
Generally, the industry has been moving away from more \textbf{restrictive} warranties (e.g. limits on number of cycles) to more \textbf{flexible} warranties (e.g. rolling averages, charge/discharge rates over the day).

\vspace{0.3cm}
Reasons for change:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Batteries mainly provide ancillary services, so applying a restriction on full cycles makes less sense
    \item Batteries earn much of their money arbitraging on a few high-demand days, rather than just once every day, so restrictions can prevent optimal dispatch
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textit{``Running a BESS with an inflexible guarantee is like paying rent all year for an umbrella shop, but being forced to keep the same daily stock; so when the rainy week comes, you sell out in the first hour and miss the payoff"} \textcolor{gray}{-Gregory Green, BlackVolt}
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\section{Literature Review and Positioning}

\begin{frame}{Literature: Electricity Storage Arbitrage}
How storage facilities optimize dispatch, how they affect market prices, and frictions preventing storage from realizing theoretical value.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Butters, Dorsey, and Gowrisankaran (2025)}: dynamic market equilibrium model of battery dispatch in CAISO; first 5,000 MWh of storage reduces wholesale prices by 5.6\%
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Their framework (dynamic programming with physical degradation costs) provides a starting point for my bidding simulation
        \item But their degradation costs are physical---warranty constraints may be substantially more restrictive
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Lamp and Samano (2022)}: document CAISO battery dispatch 2018--2019, find behavior only partially consistent with optimal arbitrage
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Attribute deviations to forecasting and strategic behavior
        \item But they don't observe warranty terms---my project asks whether warranty terms explain part of the residual
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Literature: Electricity Storage Arbitrage, cont'd}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Reynolds (2025)}: documents that 66\% of ERCOT storage is classified for arbitrage and 44\% for ancillary services (with overlap)
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Context for welfare that batteries provide
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Denholm and Margolis (2018)}: estimates storage required to meet California peaking demand under increasing solar
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Useful for situating the social stakes of dispatch inefficiency
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Literature: Contract Theory and Warranties}
How warranty terms emerge from bilateral contracting, how they allocate risk, govern moral hazard.

\vspace{0.2cm}
Prior focus: consumer durable goods---cars, appliances, electronics---where buyer usage affects product longevity.

\vspace{0.2cm}
The battery setting differs in two ways:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The buyer's dispatch decisions affect \textit{wholesale electricity market outcomes}, so warranty design has social welfare consequences beyond the bilateral relationship
    \item Unlike most consumer goods (degrading along 1--2 dimensions), batteries degrade via \textit{many} behaviors (DoD vs. cycles vs. SOC vs. C-rate, etc.), and warranty terms vary in restrictiveness across these behaviors---often depending on monitorability
\end{enumerate}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Literature: Contract Theory, cont'd}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Emons (1988)}: under asymmetric information, warranties serve as both insurance and quality signals; moral hazard induces excessive product use
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Warranty cycling restrictions are one instrument for managing this
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Emons (1989)}: longer warranties require more intensive monitoring to remain incentive-compatible
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Directly relevant to 15--20 year battery warranties, where cumulative cycle counts are both technically feasible and contractually specified
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Liu and Wang (2025)}: industry review of potentially optimal battery warranty designs from the manufacturer's perspective
    \begin{itemize}
        \item This project complements theirs by examining the \textit{market-level} implications
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Literature: Battery Degradation}
Engineering foundation for the economic analysis.

\vspace{0.2cm}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Xu et al. (2018)}: most widely used semi-empirical degradation model
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Separates aging into calendar aging (temperature, SOC level) and cycle aging (DoD, C-rate, cumulative throughput)
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Attia et al. (2022)}: document non-linear aging in lithium-ion batteries, including ``capacity knees''---abrupt inflection points where degradation accelerates
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Simple linear throughput approximations may misstate marginal degradation costs
        \item Supports conservative warranty design near the knee threshold
        \item Implies batteries operating well below the knee face lower marginal degradation costs than linear models suggest
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{My Contribution}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Unlike consumer products studied in the warranty literature, grid-scale batteries have \textbf{multiple contractable dimensions of operational behavior}---cycle frequency, DoD, C-rate, SOC
        \item These choices have potential consequences for \textit{electricity market outcomes} and welfare, not just the bilateral manufacturer-operator relationship
        \item To my knowledge, no existing economics paper has systematically examined the dispatch effects or welfare costs of warranty cycling restrictions
    \end{itemize}
    \vspace{0.3cm}
    This project aims to provide that analysis by combining tools from:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Energy economics
        \item Contract theory
        \item Battery engineering
    \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\section{Setting and Data}

\begin{frame}{Why ERCOT?}
Texas is the primary setting for this analysis.
\vspace{0.3cm}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Scale}: most installed grid-scale storage of any U.S. state (3.2 GW by early 2025, much more under construction)
    \item \textbf{Market structure}: fully deregulated, energy-only market
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Exposes batteries to relatively undistorted real-time price signals
        \item Gaps between observed and optimal dispatch more directly attributable to operational constraints than to market structure
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Price volatility}: Winter Storm Uri (2021), recurring summer scarcity events
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Creates high-value dispatch opportunities that make warranty restrictions especially costly
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Data transparency}: ERCOT 60-Day SCED disclosure provides unusually granular public data at the resource level
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Data: LBNL Berkeley Lab PPA Dataset}
Primary source of variation in warranty terms.
\vspace{0.3cm}
\begin{itemize}
    \item LBNL has a collection of approximately \textbf{105 PV-plus-battery hybrid plants} with public PPAs
    \item \textbf{Seel et al. (2021)} document operational restrictions---cycle limits, SOC requirements, DoD restrictions---disclosed as warranty conditions
    \item Because PPAs are public documents filed with state utility commissions or FERC, the warranty terms they contain are the most transparent source of cross-sectional variation across installations
    \item Status: data collection currently in progress
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Data: ERCOT SCED Disclosure}
Primary source of battery behavior data.
\vspace{0.3cm}
\begin{itemize}
    \item ERCOT's \textbf{60-Day Security Constrained Economic Dispatch (SCED) Disclosure Report}
    \item Resource-level, 5-minute interval dispatch data for every generator and energy storage resource since January 2011
    \item Key variables:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Telemetered output and load (actual MW)
        \item Submitted energy offer curves (full price-quantity pairs)
        \item Ancillary service awards across all ERCOT products
    \end{itemize}
    \item Crucially, releases the \textit{bids} that resources submit for energy and ancillary services---a useful outcome variable in descriptive analyses
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Data: Linking and Supplementary Sources}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{EIA Form 860/923}: plant-level identification (name, operator, location, capacity, technology) to link the PPA dataset to ERCOT SCED
    \vspace{0.2cm}
    \item \textbf{Potential industry sources}:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item NEC Energy Solutions, IronGrid---companies specializing in adaptive battery warranties
        \item May provide structured warranty data for standalone ERCOT storage units not in LBNL sample
        \item May also provide examples of batteries that modified warranties over time (useful for DiD)
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\section{Proposed Empirical Strategies}

\begin{frame}{Empirical Strategy: Overview}
Data collection is currently in progress; no analyses yet. Three planned empirical exercises:
\vspace{0.3cm}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Descriptive comparisons across warranty types}: document dispatch differences across the cross-section of batteries
    \item \textbf{Difference-in-differences}: exploit manufacturer-level warranty policy changes
    \item \textbf{Battery dispatch simulation}: bound welfare magnitudes under different warranty regimes
\end{enumerate}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Strategy 1: Descriptive Comparisons}
First step: systematically categorize PPA-disclosed warranty terms into ``more'' vs. ``less'' restrictive types.
\vspace{0.2cm}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Compare distributions of:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Daily cycle counts
        \item Ancillary service participation rates
        \item Discharge response to unexpected price spikes
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Price spike definition}: settlement point prices $>3\sigma$ above historical hour mean, associated with unforecasted supply outages (not weather shocks already in operator forecasts)
    \item \textbf{Predictions}:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Cycle-restricted batteries will show lower discharge intensity during price spikes
        \item Lower ancillary service award rates relative to physical capacity
        \item Effect concentrated later in the year when cycle budgets are most depleted
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Strategy 1: Identification Concerns}
\textbf{Central challenge}: batteries with more restrictive warranties may differ from less-restricted ones along many dimensions.
\vspace{0.2cm}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Ownership, technology, vintage, market position
    \item Larger/more sophisticated operators may negotiate more flexible warranty terms \textit{and} dispatch more efficiently for unrelated reasons
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
The descriptive analysis documents these potential confounds systematically. The DiD design (next) partially addresses them by holding the battery fixed.

\vspace{0.3cm}
Even as purely descriptive, the cross-section reveals whether variation in the data is rich enough to support causal analyses.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Strategy 2: Difference-in-Differences}
Several manufacturers shifted from rigid to flexible warranty structures between 2023 and 2025.
\vspace{0.2cm}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Candidate treatment event: Trina Storage's early-2025 adoption of rolling-average flexible warranties
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Replaced a fixed-cycle-count regime with a continuous C-rate cap
    \end{itemize}
    \item Access to NEC/IronGrid data would expand the set of ``treated'' batteries
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{DiD design}: Compare cycling intensity, ancillary service participation, and extreme-event dispatch response between:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Treated: batteries from manufacturers that revised their warranties
    \item Controls: batteries from manufacturers that did not
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Strategy 2: Identifying Assumption and Backup}
\textbf{Key identifying assumption}: parallel pre-treatment trends in dispatch behavior.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Fallback if parallel trends fail}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item An event study for a single battery comparing dispatch before vs. after its warranty revision
    \item Less generalizable, but still potentially informative about the within-battery response to relaxing contractual constraints
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
Effectively absorbs time-invariant differences across installations that the cross-sectional analysis cannot.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Strategy 3: Battery Dispatch Simulation}
To get a rough sense of welfare magnitudes, solve the optimal dispatch problem under four warranty regimes:
\vspace{0.2cm}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Unconstrained}: physical degradation costs only (calibrated from Xu et al., 2018)
    \item \textbf{1-cycle-per-day limit}
    \item \textbf{Annual throughput cap} equivalent to 365 cycles
    \item \textbf{0.25C power cap}
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
Algorithm predicts prices given observable information (previous period price, day-ahead price) and solves optimal bidding.

\vspace{0.3cm}
Produces \textit{upper-bound} estimate of profit loss attributable to each type of restriction, under the assumption operators otherwise dispatch optimally.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Strategy 3: Connection to the Empirical Design}
    The simulation and empirical strategies complement each other:
    \vspace{0.2cm}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Empirical analyses} (Strategies 1 \& 2) identify whether warranty restrictions distort dispatch in practice
        \item \textbf{Simulation} (Strategy 3) asks \textit{how much} of the observed gap is predicted by the constraint itself, and bounds the maximum welfare loss
        \item If simulated and empirical effects align, gives confidence the mechanism is warranty-driven
        \item If empirical effects exceed simulated ones, points to forward-looking behavior or strategic conservation of cycles beyond what the hard constraint requires
    \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\section{Welfare Analysis}

\begin{frame}{Potential Welfare Analysis}
The social cost of warranty-induced distortions decomposes into three components:
\vspace{0.2cm}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Foregone arbitrage profits}---the private cost to the battery operator
    \item \textbf{Lost ancillary service value}---shadow price of frequency regulation and spinning reserves withheld due to restrictions
    \item \textbf{Environmental externalities}---increased fossil peaker dispatch that replaces constrained battery output
\end{enumerate}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Component 1: Foregone Arbitrage Profits}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Computed per SCED interval as:
    \[
    \text{LMP}_t \times (q^*_{t,\text{unconstrained}} - q_{t,\text{warranty-constrained}})
    \]
    \item Minus degradation costs calculated from engineering models (Xu et al., 2018)
    \vspace{0.3cm}
    \item This is the private cost---what operators would be willing to pay to relax the warranty constraint
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Component 2: Lost Ancillary Service Value}
Capturing the shadow price of frequency regulation and spinning reserve capacity withheld due to warranty restrictions.
\vspace{0.2cm}
\begin{itemize}
    \item ERCOT ancillary service market clearing prices are directly observable from SCED data
    \item Provides market-based valuations for foregone regulatory services
    \item \textbf{Frequency regulation} requires precisely the rapid cycling that cycle-count warranties restrict
    \item Ancillary services account for a substantial share of battery revenues in ERCOT
    \item Expect this component to be quantitatively significant---though magnitude is an empirical question
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Component 3: Environmental Externalities}
If constrained batteries force greater reliance on fossil fuel peakers.
\vspace{0.2cm}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Follow the merit-order counterfactual approach of:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Cicala (2022)
        \item Gonzales, Ito, and Reguant (2023)
    \end{itemize}
    \item Compute marginal emissions rate of the relevant fossil resource during periods when batteries would have dispatched under the unconstrained optimum but did not
    \item Multiply by the social cost of carbon
    \vspace{0.3cm}
    \item This captures the social cost that goes beyond private losses borne by operators
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\section{Conclusion}

\begin{frame}{Summary}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Grid-scale batteries are valued for their flexibility in electricity markets
    \item But performance warranties impose binding operational restrictions that may substantially distort dispatch
    \item Central hypothesis: \textbf{contractual frictions---not only physical degradation or market power---represent a first-order barrier to efficient battery dispatch}
    \item These frictions impose social costs beyond the private losses borne by operators
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
Rigid warranty structures may systematically undermine the flexibility value that justifies investment in grid-scale storage.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{What I Hope to Contribute}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Document and categorize} operational restrictions embedded in performance warranties using PPA-disclosed warranty terms
    \item \textbf{Describe the relationship} between warranty restrictiveness and dispatch behavior, exploiting cross-sectional variation and temporal variation from manufacturer policy shifts
    \item \textbf{Quantify the welfare cost}, decomposing losses into foregone arbitrage profits, lost ancillary services, and fossil emissions externalities
\end{enumerate}
\vspace{0.3cm}
Broader aim: improve our understanding of battery dispatch decisions and constraints in practice, and of the benefits of flexible warranty design in certain markets.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Open Questions for Discussion}
\begin{itemize}
    \item How to handle endogeneity of warranty choice (larger operators may negotiate better terms)?
    \item Is a structural demand-side model of warranty contracting worth building, or is reduced-form evidence sufficient?
    \item Are there additional natural experiments---beyond manufacturer policy changes---that could help identification?
    \item What's the right counterfactual dispatch benchmark: physical degradation, socially optimal, or profit-maximizing?
    \item Generalizability: do results from ERCOT extend to CAISO, PJM, or regulated markets?
\end{itemize}
